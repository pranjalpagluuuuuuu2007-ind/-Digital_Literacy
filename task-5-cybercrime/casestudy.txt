Case Study: The UPI "Receive Money" Trap
What it is:
UPI scams, particularly the QR code trick, are becoming incredibly common on campus. The scary part is how simple it is: fraudsters trick you into scanning a QR code by making it look like you’re about to receive a payment. In reality, the moment you enter your UPI PIN, you aren't receiving anything—you’re authorizing a withdrawal. Because UPI is instantaneous, once that PIN is entered, the money is gone before you even realize what happened.

How it typically happens:
It usually starts with something innocent, like selling an old textbook or a cycle on OLX or Facebook Marketplace. A "buyer" messages you, acting super interested and in a huge rush to close the deal. To make it seem convenient, they’ll send a QR code over WhatsApp and say, “Hey, just scan this to claim the advance payment.”

The seller, just wanting to get the sale over with, scans the code and enters their PIN, expecting to see their balance go up. Instead, they get a notification that their account has been debited. It’s a gut-wrenching feeling of panic the second you realize you’ve been duped.

Who is usually targeted:
Scammers specifically target students and first-time online sellers. They rely on our generation's comfort with fast technology and our desire for a "hassle-free" transaction. They use urgency to stop you from thinking twice.

The Consequences:
The most obvious hit is the financial loss; since you authorized the transaction with a PIN, banks find it nearly impossible to reverse the charge. Beyond the money, there’s a real emotional toll—feeling embarrassed or anxious about being "tricked." Ultimately, it ruins the trust we have in digital tools that are supposed to make our lives easier.

The Takeaway:
The golden rule of UPI is: You never, ever need to enter your PIN to receive money. If someone asks you to "scan and enter PIN" to get paid, it is 100% a scam. Stay skeptical, especially when someone is rushing you.
